# Contract: product-foundation

**A project defines its product and its users — a durable brief — before it builds features, through GENUINE discovery: gather the product's real zero-to-working story prejudice-free, then conclude — able, at the end, to conclude "we built the wrong thing." Every feature then grounds in that brief.**

Onboarding wires the machinery (config, models, tools) but never establishes *what product, and for whom* — so features get built for who-knows-why, churning code instead of building a product. This inserts the missing first step: a product-discovery dialog that records a durable brief (`docs/product.md`) — the **idea** (the anchor every later question reasons around), the **customer**, the **problem** in their words, the concrete **zero-to-working story** (how a user discovers it, onboards from nothing, carries across devices and recovers lost access), the **competition / incumbent**, the **viability** (who runs and funds it), and — at the end — the **case against**. Grounded in the established discovery frameworks (*Working Backwards*, *Lean Canvas*, Cagan's four risks, Torres): customer and problem first, the competition named explicitly, a case-against built in. The brief is the single home for who/why; the loop's Understand beat and the per-feature product questions ground in it rather than re-deciding who/why each time.

## Must work

- Product discovery runs at onboarding (right after `setup`) and is offered — a short, declinable offer — on the first feature request to a configured project with no brief; it is also runnable on demand.
- Discovery **anchors on the idea** (brief section 0) and reasons AROUND it: every later question's options are plausible *given* the product, never generic or absurd (for an AI software/docs protocol the customer is a PM/developer/founder/student, not an auto-mechanic). No scope-gate theater — arriving at the protocol already implies a software/docs product. The idea is a seed problem-first discovery may reshape or reject, not a lock.
- The brief's spine is the concrete **zero-to-working story** — how a user discovers the product, onboards from nothing to working, carries across devices/sessions, recovers lost access, and who runs/funds it — questions a non-expert can actually answer, preferred over abstract prompts ("name your riskiest assumption").
- The **competition is named**, not skipped — what the customer uses today and how this differs; if the others are unknown, discovery **researches them first** (the `research` side-tool) and drafts from the research before asking the Operator to correct it — never "who are your competitors?" to an Operator who does not know the landscape.
- Discovery **gathers prejudice-free, then concludes** — the two phases are not mixed: while gathering it is a **gap detector, not a judge** (records what the Operator gives, marks the rest `[?]`, never grades an answer's quality nor plants risk/trap flags mid-stream), and the **case against** is weighed at the END, on top of what was gathered — never a leading question up front. Each round is a **different kind** of inquiry; the customer is asked **openly** (often a spectrum, not a pick-one), never a manufactured fork on a range.
- The brief is recorded in its single home (`docs/product.md`) and is the one place who/why lives — features point to it, never restate it.
- A feature plan and the product-advocate product questions check the change against the brief — does it serve the product and user the brief names — not in isolation.

## Must not break

- The orchestrator never **retrofits** the brief onto what is already built — discovery gathers the user's story from the ground up, never reverse-engineered from the shipped product. A discovery shaped to confirm the existing product is theater, not this contract.
- The gathering phase **does not pre-judge** — grading an answer's quality, planting "risk/trap" flags, or forcing an exclusion before anything is researched contaminates the data; the weighing belongs to the end. (The Operator owns meaning; the orchestrator detects gaps.)
- A discovery that **cannot conclude "wrong product / cut / pivot"** is not this contract. The procedure must license — and require honesty about — reporting that the answers do not fit what was built; an invalidation-incapable rubber stamp is a contract break.
- When options are offered (chiefly at the case-against conclusion) they are genuinely **different directions** with their tradeoffs, never flattering variations of one — and never a fake fork on an axis that is really a spectrum.
- The orchestrator never invents the product — it asks the Operator (the same honesty as setup never inventing a model id); a number the Operator has not fixed is `[?]`, never guessed.
- Discovery is an offer, not a block — declinable like the lazy-setup offer; the nudge only reinforces a `[persona]` act and never forces it (the enforcement floor cannot force a positive act).
- The brief is kept current, not a one-time throwaway — a change that shifts who/why updates it, the same way a behavioural change updates its contract.
